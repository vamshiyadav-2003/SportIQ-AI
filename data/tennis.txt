Tennis Knowledge Base & Historical Facts

1. Tennis is a racquet sport that can be played individually against a single opponent (singles) or between two teams of two players each (doubles).
2. The modern game of tennis originated in Birmingham, England, in the late 19th century as "lawn tennis," which was closely related to the ancient game of real tennis.
3. Real tennis (or royal tennis) was played indoors since the 12th century, particularly in France, where it was popular among royalty, including King Louis X, who died after a game.
4. The name "tennis" is derived from the French word "tenez," which means "hold" or "receive," a cry used by servers to warn their opponents that they were about to serve.
5. Major Walter Clopton Wingfield is credited with patenting the rules of lawn tennis in 1874, designing an hour-glass shaped court.
6. The first official tennis tournament was held at the All England Croquet and Lawn Tennis Club in Wimbledon, London, in July 1877. This inaugural Wimbledon Championship featured only a men's singles event, won by Spencer Gore.
7. The four major annual tennis tournaments, known as the "Grand Slams" or "Majors," are Wimbledon, the US Open, the French Open, and the Australian Open.
8. The term "Grand Slam" refers to winning all four major tournaments in a single calendar year.
9. Don Budge of the USA was the first player to achieve a calendar-year Grand Slam, doing so in 1938.
10. Rod Laver of Australia is the only player in tennis history to achieve the calendar-year Grand Slam twice in men's singles, doing so in 1962 (as an amateur) and 1969 (as a professional).
11. Margaret Court of Australia holds the all-time record for the most Grand Slam singles titles in history, winning 24 titles (11 in the amateur era, 13 in the Open Era).
12. Margaret Court achieved the calendar-year Grand Slam in women's singles in 1970.
13. Novak Djokovic of Serbia holds the men's record for the most Grand Slam singles titles in history, winning 24 titles.
14. Djokovic has won the Australian Open a record 10 times, the most by any male player.
15. Rafael Nadal of Spain, nicknamed "The King of Clay," has won 22 Grand Slam singles titles, including a record 14 French Open (Roland Garros) titles.
16. Nadal's 14 titles at a single Grand Slam is the most dominant record in tennis history, losing only 4 matches out of 116 played at the French Open.
17. Roger Federer of Switzerland won 20 Grand Slam singles titles, including a record 8 Wimbledon men's singles titles.
18. Federer held the world number one ranking for a record 237 consecutive weeks between 2004 and 2008.
19. The "Big Three" of men's tennis (Djokovic, Nadal, and Federer) dominated the sport for nearly two decades, winning 66 out of 81 Grand Slams between 2003 and 2023.
20. Serena Williams of the USA holds the Open Era record for the most Grand Slam singles titles, winning 23 titles.
21. Serena and her sister Venus Williams established a famous rivalry and also won 14 Grand Slam doubles titles together, remaining undefeated in Grand Slam doubles finals.
22. Steffi Graf of Germany won 22 Grand Slam singles titles and is the only player (male or female) to achieve the "Golden Slam" by winning all four Grand Slams and an Olympic gold medal in the same calendar year (1988).
23. Graf held the world number one ranking for a record total of 377 weeks, a record later surpassed by Novak Djokovic (who exceeded 400 weeks).
24. The "Open Era" of tennis began in 1968 when major tournaments agreed to allow professional players to compete against amateurs. Previously, professionals were banned from Grand Slams.
25. The International Tennis Federation (ITF) is the global governing body of tennis, founded in Paris in 1913 as the International Lawn Tennis Federation (ILTF) and currently based in London.
26. The ATP (Association of Tennis Professionals) was formed in 1972 to protect the interests of male professional players, introducing the world ranking system in 1973.
27. The WTA (Women's Tennis Association) was founded in 1973 by Billie Jean King to unite women's professional tennis, leading to equal prize money at major tournaments.
28. Billie Jean King won the famous "Battle of the Sexes" match in 1973, defeating former men's champion Bobby Riggs in three straight sets in Houston, Texas, before a crowd of over 30,000 spectators.
29. The court dimensions for a singles tennis match are 78 feet (23.77 meters) long by 27 feet (8.23 meters) wide.
30. For doubles matches, the court width is increased to 36 feet (10.97 meters) by including the "doubles alleys."
31. The net is 3 feet 6 inches (1.07 meters) high at the posts and 3 feet (0.914 meters) high at the center, held down by a central strap.
32. The service line is located 21 feet (6.40 meters) from the net on each side, determining the boundaries of the service box.
33. Tennis is played on four main types of court surfaces: Grass (fastest, low bounce, used at Wimbledon), Clay (slowest, high bounce, used at the French Open), Hard court (medium-fast, true bounce, used at US and Australian Opens), and Carpet (phased out of professional play).
34. The French Open is the only Grand Slam played on red clay courts, which are actually made of crushed white brick dust topped on limestone.
35. Wimbledon is the only Grand Slam still played on natural grass, which is kept cut to an exact height of 8 millimeters.
36. The scoring system in tennis uses the terms "Love" (0), "15", "30", "40", and "Game."
37. The origin of the term "Love" in tennis scoring is believed to come from the French word "l'oeuf," which means "the egg," representing the shape of a zero.
38. If both players reach a score of 40-40, it is called "Deuce." The player who scores the next point gains the "Advantage" (Ad), and must score another consecutive point to win the game. If they lose the next point, the score returns to Deuce.
39. To win a set, a player must win at least 6 games and lead by a margin of 2 games (e.g., 6-4).
40. If a set reaches a 6-6 tie, a "tiebreaker" game is played. The first player to reach 7 points (and lead by 2 points) wins the set 7-6.
41. In 2022, all four Grand Slams unified their rules to play a 10-point tiebreaker in the final set when the score reaches 6-6.
42. Previously, final sets at Wimbledon and the French Open had to be won by a margin of 2 games, leading to extremely long matches.
43. The longest match in tennis history took place at Wimbledon in 2010 between John Isner of the USA and Nicolas Mahut of France. The match lasted 11 hours and 5 minutes over three days, ending in a fifth-set score of 70-68 in favor of Isner.
44. The fastest recorded serve in men's tennis history belongs to Samuel Groth of Australia, who hit a serve at 263.4 km/h (163.7 mph) at a Challenger event in Busan in 2012.
45. John Isner holds the official ATP record for the fastest serve, clocked at 253 km/h (157.2 mph) during a Davis Cup match in 2016.
46. Sabine Lisicki of Germany holds the record for the fastest serve in women's tennis, clocked at 210.8 km/h (131 mph) at Stanford in 2014.
47. Pete Sampras of the USA dominated the 1990s, winning 14 Grand Slam singles titles and finishing as year-end world number one for 6 consecutive years (1993-1998).
48. Björn Borg of Sweden won 11 Grand Slam titles (5 consecutive Wimbledons and 6 French Opens) before retiring abruptly at age 26.
49. Borg was famous for his cool temperament, earning him the nickname "Ice Borg."
50. Jimmy Connors of the USA holds the Open Era record for the most men's singles titles (109) and the most matches won (1,274).
51. Chris Evert and Martina Navratilova established one of the greatest rivalries in women's sports history, playing each other 80 times (Navratilova led 43-37) and winning 18 Grand Slam singles titles each.
52. Navratilova holds the record for the most Wimbledon singles titles, winning 9 titles.
53. Navratilova also holds the all-time Open Era record for the most singles titles (167) and doubles titles (177).
54. The Davis Cup, first held in 1900, is the premier international men's team tennis competition.
55. The Billie Jean King Cup (formerly Federation Cup or Fed Cup), established in 1963, is the premier international women's team tennis competition.
56. An "ace" is a legal serve that lands in the service box and is completely untouched by the receiver's racquet, scoring a point.
57. John Isner holds the career record for the most aces served in professional tennis, hitting over 14,000 aces.
58. A "double fault" occurs when a server misses both their first and second serves during a point, awarding a point to the receiver.
59. A "let" is called if a serve touches the net band but still lands in the correct service box. The serve does not count and must be replayed.
60. The "Hawk-Eye" line-calling system was first introduced in professional tennis in 2006, using high-speed cameras to track the ball's trajectory and determine if it landed in or out.
61. In 2021, the Australian Open became the first Grand Slam to replace all on-court line judges with "Electronic Line Calling Live" (Hawk-Eye Live), which announces calls in real time using automated voices.
62. The "shot clock" was introduced to speed up play, giving players a maximum of 25 seconds to serve between points.
63. Players are allowed to challenge line calls. If the electronic review shows the challenge was incorrect, the player loses one of their challenges (they are allowed 3 per set, plus 1 in a tiebreaker).
64. An "unforced error" is a mistake made by a player on a relatively easy shot that is not under pressure from the opponent.
65. A "winner" is a shot that lands in bounds and is not touched by the opponent, scoring a point.
66. Tennis balls are hollow rubber cores covered in felt (wool/nylon blend) and filled with pressurized gas to make them bouncy.
67. Yellow tennis balls were first introduced at Wimbledon in 1986 to make them more visible on color televisions; previously, white balls were standard.
68. Tennis balls are kept in pressurized cans until use to prevent them from losing their gas pressure.
69. During professional matches, balls are replaced after the first 7 games (to allow for warm-up wear) and then after every 9 games.
70. The "warm-up" before a match is limited to exactly 5 minutes, allowing players to hit balls with each other before play begins.
71. A player is not allowed to touch the net or cross their racquet over the net during a rally; doing so results in losing the point.
72. A "foot fault" occurs if the server steps on or over the baseline before hitting the ball during their service motion.
73. The "grand slam" of tennis has been completed in a calendar year by only 5 players across all categories (singles, doubles, juniors).
74. Rod Laver won the Grand Slam in 1969, and no male player has completed a calendar-year Grand Slam in singles since.
75. Novak Djokovic came close in 2021, winning the Australian Open, French Open, and Wimbledon, but lost the US Open final to Daniil Medvedev.
76. Monica Seles won 9 Grand Slam singles titles and was famous for her two-handed forehand and backhand, before her career was interrupted when she was stabbed on court by a fan of Steffi Graf in Hamburg in 1993.
77. Seles returned to tennis two years later and won the 1996 Australian Open.
78. Arthur Ashe was the first African American male player to win the US Open (1968) and Wimbledon (1975). The main stadium at the US Open is named Arthur Ashe Stadium in his honor, serving as the largest tennis stadium in the world.
79. Billie Jean King was a key organizer of the "Original Nine" players who broke away from the tennis establishment to form the women's professional tour in 1970, which led to the creation of the WTA.
80. The first US Open was held in 1881 at Newport, Rhode Island, and was played on grass courts.
81. The US Open is the only Grand Slam that has been played on three different surfaces: Grass (1881-1974), Clay (1975-1977), and Hard court (1978-present).
82. The Australian Open, first held in 1905, was played on grass courts until 1987, when it moved to Flinders Park (now Melbourne Park) and switched to hard courts.
83. Ken Rosewall of Australia is both the youngest (at age 18 in 1953) and oldest (at age 37 in 1972) male player to win the Australian Open singles title.
84. Yannick Noah was the last French male player to win the French Open singles title, doing so in 1983.
85. The "Davis Cup" was originally founded by Dwight F. Davis, a Harvard university tennis player who designed the tournament format and purchased the sterling silver trophy.
86. A "lob" is a high, arching shot hit over an opponent who has approached the net, forcing them to run backward.
87. A "passing shot" is a drive hit past an opponent at the net, out of their reach.
88. The "volley" is a shot hit in the air before the ball bounces on the court, usually executed near the net.
89. The "half-volley" is a shot hit immediately after the ball bounces, very low to the ground.
90. The "overhead smash" is a powerful shot hit down from above the head, similar to a serve, usually in response to a short lob.
91. A player must win two points in a row after reaching Deuce to win the game. If they win one point, they hold "Advantage"; if they lose the next, it goes back to Deuce.
92. Professional matches are best-of-three sets for women's singles, and best-of-five sets for men's singles at Grand Slam tournaments. In all other ATP tournaments, men's matches are best-of-three sets.
93. A "code violation" can be issued by the chair umpire for racket abuse, ball abuse, audible obscenity, visible obscenity, or coaching.
94. The warning system for code violations is: Warning (first offense), Point Penalty (second offense), Game Penalty (third offense), and Default/Disqualification (fourth offense).
95. John McEnroe was famous for his volatile behavior, screaming "You cannot be serious!" at a Wimbledon chair umpire in 1981, which became his catchphrase.
96. McEnroe won 7 Grand Slam singles titles and 9 Grand Slam doubles titles, and was famous for his touch and serve-and-volley style.
97. The "walkover" occurs when a player advances to the next round because their opponent withdraws before the match begins due to injury or illness.
98. A "retirement" occurs when a player is unable to continue playing during a match, awarding the victory to their opponent.
99. Ilie Nastase of Romania, nicknamed "The Bucharest Buffoon" for his eccentric behavior, was the first player to be ranked world number one on the official ATP computer rankings in 1973.
100. Tennis returned to the Olympic Games as a full medal sport at the 1988 Seoul Olympics, after being excluded since 1924.
